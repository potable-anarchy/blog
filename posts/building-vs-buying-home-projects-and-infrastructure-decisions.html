Building vs. Buying: Home Projects and Infrastructure Decisions

Last weekend, I found myself staring at a leaky kitchen faucet, calculator in hand, trying to decide whether to fix it myself or call a plumber. The decision-making process felt surprisingly familiar—it was the same mental framework I use when choosing between building custom infrastructure tools versus buying commercial solutions.

The $8 Washer vs. the $200 Service Call

The faucet was dripping because of a worn-out O-ring that costs $0.50 at the hardware store. But fixing it meant turning off the water main, dismantling the faucet assembly, and hoping I could get everything back together without flooding the kitchen. The plumber would charge $200 but guarantee the work.

This is exactly the calculation we make with infrastructure decisions. Do we spend weeks building a custom monitoring dashboard, or do we pay for DataDog? Do we roll our own deployment pipeline, or do we use GitHub Actions? The math isn't just about money—it's about time, expertise, and risk.

When DIY Makes Sense

I decided to fix the faucet myself because I had the tools, the time, and most importantly, the failure mode was manageable. Worst case, I'd still need to call the plumber, but I'd only be out a few hours and the cost of the parts.

Similarly, we built our own log aggregation system because we had unique requirements that off-the-shelf solutions couldn't handle. Our data volumes were massive, our budget was tight, and we had the engineering bandwidth to maintain it. The risk was contained—if it failed, we could always fall back to cloud logging services.

The Hidden Costs of "Simple" Projects

The faucet repair took four hours instead of the estimated one hour. The O-ring wasn't the only problem—the valve seat was corroded, requiring a special tool I didn't own. By the time I finished, I'd made three trips to the hardware store and spent $40 on tools I'd probably never use again.

Our "simple" logging system followed the same pattern. What started as a weekend project became a three-month endeavor. We underestimated the complexity of handling log rotation, search indexing, and multi-tenant access controls. The hidden costs weren't just development time—they were the opportunity cost of features we didn't build while we were playing infrastructure engineer.

The Expertise Question

Here's what I learned about my plumbing skills: I can handle basic repairs, but I'm not a plumber. When the garbage disposal started making grinding noises, I called a professional immediately. I know my limits.

The same principle applies to infrastructure decisions. We're good at building web applications, but we're not experts in database internals or networking protocols. When we needed a message queue, we chose AWS SQS over building our own. When we needed a CDN, we went with CloudFlare instead of trying to optimize our own edge caching.

The Maintenance Reality

Six months after fixing the faucet, it started leaking again. Different problem, same faucet. This time I called the plumber, who explained that the entire valve assembly was worn out and needed replacement. "You did good work on the O-ring," he said, "but this faucet is just old."

Maintenance is the hidden cost of every "build" decision. That custom logging system we built? It needed constant tuning, security updates, and feature additions as our needs evolved. The commercial alternatives we dismissed for being "too expensive" included maintenance, security, and feature development in their pricing.

The Decision Framework

Now I approach both home repairs and infrastructure decisions with the same framework:

  • Do we have the right expertise? If the answer is "we'll figure it out," that's a red flag.
  • What's the blast radius of failure? A kitchen flood is annoying. A production outage is career-limiting.
  • What's the total cost of ownership? Include development time, maintenance, and opportunity costs.
  • Is this a competitive advantage? Custom faucets don't differentiate our home. Custom infrastructure rarely differentiates our product.

The Sweet Spot

The best decisions happen when you can clearly articulate why you're choosing to build instead of buy. We built our deployment pipeline because we had specific requirements around security and compliance that commercial solutions couldn't meet. We knew exactly what we needed, we had the expertise to build it, and the maintenance overhead was manageable.

Similarly, I've gotten better at home repairs by focusing on projects where I can learn something useful and the stakes are low. I installed smart switches throughout the house, not because it was cheaper than hiring an electrician, but because I wanted to understand how they work and the failure mode was just a dark room.

Build vs. Buy vs. Learn

Sometimes the real value isn't in the solution itself, but in the learning process. Building that first logging system taught us about distributed systems, data pipelines, and operational complexity. When we eventually migrated to a commercial solution, we were much better customers because we understood the problem space.

The same thing happened with home projects. Fixing that faucet taught me about plumbing basics, which made me a better homeowner overall. I still call professionals for major work, but now I can have informed conversations about what needs to be done and why.

Whether you're deciding between a $200 plumber and a $8 part, or between a $50,000 commercial solution and a custom build, the framework is the same: understand your constraints, know your limits, and factor in the hidden costs. The best engineers—and the best homeowners—know when to build, when to buy, and when to call in the experts.
