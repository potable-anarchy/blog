March 13, 2025 Incident Management

Musical Improvisation and Incident Response

The API went down at 2:13 AM during peak traffic from our European users. No clear root cause, cascading failures, and a junior engineer on call who was three espressos past rational thought. This is when I remembered what my old jazz piano teacher, Mrs. Rodriguez, taught me about improvisation: "When you don't know where you're going, listen to where you are."

Twenty-three years later, those words guide me through every production incident.

Finding the Changes

In jazz, every improvisation starts with the changes—the underlying chord progression that gives structure to creativity. You can venture far from the melody, but you need to understand the harmonic foundation first.

When I walked into my first incident war room eight years ago, the senior engineer on duty asked me, "What are the changes?" I had no idea what he meant. Our service was failing, users were angry, and he was asking about music theory?

He pulled up our architecture diagram. "These are our changes," he said, pointing to the data flow between services. "Payment service talks to user service talks to notification service. That's our progression. Now, which chord is wrong?"

I've used that metaphor ever since. Before diving into logs or restarting services, identify your changes—the core data flows, dependencies, and assumptions your system relies on. Once you understand the progression, you can hear which service is playing out of tune.

The Art of Comping

In jazz piano, comping is the art of playing chord voicings that support other musicians without getting in their way. You're essential to the sound, but you're not the melody.

During incidents, most people want to be the soloist—the hero who finds the fix. But the best incident response feels like good comping: everyone playing their part, creating space for others, supporting the overall rhythm of resolution.

Last month, our database started throwing timeout errors during a product launch. Instead of everyone diving into the same logs, we naturally fell into roles:

  • Sarah comped with user impact assessment—monitoring support tickets and user-facing metrics
  • Miguel comped with infrastructure—checking load balancers, network latency, basic health metrics
  • I comped with application logic—tracing requests through our code to find bottlenecks

No one assigned these roles. We'd practiced enough together to know how to support each other. Miguel found the issue—a misconfigured connection pool—but it only took eight minutes because Sarah and I provided the harmonic foundation he needed to work efficiently.

Trading Fours

"Trading fours" is when musicians take turns improvising for four-bar phrases, building on what came before. It's collaborative creativity under pressure—exactly like debugging complex issues.

During a particularly gnarly performance degradation incident, my teammate Jake and I started trading fours naturally:

Jake (4 bars): "CPU looks normal, memory looks normal, but response times are spiking every thirty seconds."

Me (4 bars): "Thirty seconds... that's suspicious. Let me check our background job schedules. Oh wait—image processing queue is backing up."

Jake (4 bars): "Right, and image processing hits the file system hard. But why would that affect API response times? Different services."

Me (4 bars): "Unless... are we sharing database connections? Check the connection pool monitoring."

Bingo. Our image processing jobs were exhausting the database connection pool, starving the API. Each four-bar trade built on the previous insight, creating a melody line that led us to the root cause.

Playing Outside

Jazz musicians sometimes "play outside"—deliberately using notes that don't fit the underlying chords to create tension and interest. It sounds wrong, but in context, it creates something beautiful.

The best engineers I know aren't afraid to play outside during incidents. When conventional debugging approaches aren't working, they try things that shouldn't work but sometimes do.

"What if we just... restart the load balancer?" my colleague Emma suggested during a mysterious traffic routing issue. It was completely unrelated to our investigation, but we'd exhausted logical approaches. Sometimes you need to play a sharp eleven over a major seven chord and see what happens.

It worked. Still don't know why, but our incident post-mortem included the note: "Sometimes percussion fixes melody problems."

The Rhythm Section

In any jazz ensemble, the rhythm section (bass and drums) keeps everyone together. They're not flashy, but without them, the music falls apart.

Incident response needs a rhythm section: the monitoring systems, communication channels, and people who keep everyone synchronized. During high-stress situations, these steady patterns become essential.

Our incident response rhythm section:

  • Bass line (monitoring): Consistent heartbeat of system health metrics
  • Drums (communication): Regular status updates, even when there's no news
  • Timekeeper (incident commander): Someone who watches the clock and calls changes

Without the rhythm section, even talented soloists sound chaotic. With it, a group of decent engineers can sound like masters.

Listening While Playing

The hardest part of improvisation isn't playing notes—it's listening to what everyone else is playing and responding in real time. You have to split your attention between your own performance and the collective sound.

During incidents, I see engineers who can diagnose complex problems in isolation but fall apart in group debugging sessions. They're so focused on their own investigation that they miss crucial information from teammates.

The best incident responders I know have developed the jazz musician's ability to listen while playing. They can trace through application logs while simultaneously processing status updates from other engineers, adjusting their investigation based on new information.

It's a skill that takes practice. I recommend pair debugging sessions during non-critical times—not just for knowledge transfer, but to develop the musical skill of playing with others.

The Head Out

Jazz standards usually end by playing the "head"—the original melody—one more time before the final chord. After all the improvisation and exploration, you return to the theme that started everything.

Good incident response has the same structure. After all the creative debugging and outside-the-box thinking, you end by returning to fundamentals: Is the system working for users? Are we monitoring the right things? What can we improve for next time?

The head out for every incident is the same simple melody: verify the fix, communicate clearly with users, and learn something for next time. No matter how complex the improvisation gets, always end with the head.

Practicing Your Scales

Jazz musicians practice scales not to play them in performance, but to have that muscle memory available when they need it. When you're improvising at 200 BPM, you don't have time to think about which notes fit which chords.

The best incident responders practice their scales too:

  • Regular chaos engineering exercises
  • Tabletop incident scenarios
  • Post-incident reviews that focus on process, not just technical fixes
  • Cross-training so everyone knows basic patterns in different systems

When your API is down and users are angry, you don't want to be figuring out how to read database slow query logs for the first time. That knowledge should be muscle memory.

The Real Book

Every jazz musician owns a copy of "The Real Book"—a collection of lead sheets for jazz standards. It's not sheet music with every note written out, just the essential information: melody, chords, and structure. The rest you improvise.

Our incident runbooks should be like The Real Book. Not step-by-step procedures that kill creative thinking, but essential information that gives people freedom to improvise: service dependencies, key metrics to watch, common failure patterns, and escalation contacts.

The goal isn't to script every possible response. It's to give people enough structure to support their improvisation when things go wrong.

Encore: The Standards Never Get Old

Jazz musicians play the same standards hundreds of times, but each performance is unique. The chord changes might be the same, but the improvisation is always different.

It's the same with incident response. Database performance issues, network partitions, deployment failures—these are our standards. We've seen them before, but each incident has its own unique characteristics that require fresh thinking.

The beauty is in the improvisation within structure, the creativity within constraints, the collaboration under pressure. Just like jazz, it's about listening to your bandmates and creating something together that none of you could create alone.

Now if you'll excuse me, my monitoring dashboard is showing some interesting chord changes that need investigation.
