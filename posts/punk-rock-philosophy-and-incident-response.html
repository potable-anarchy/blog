May 26, 2025 Incident Management

Punk Rock Philosophy and Incident Response

It's 3 AM and our payment processing system is down. Users are flooding our support channels. Revenue is bleeding. The on-call engineer is freaking out. This is when I reach for my mental playbook—not the official incident response document, but something I learned from a Dead Kennedys show twenty years ago.

"DIY or die," Jello Biafra once screamed into a microphone, and I've never found a better motto for incident response.

Three Chords and the Truth

I discovered punk in 1994, sitting in my friend's basement listening to Minor Threat's "Straight Edge" on repeat. The music was raw, immediate, and unapologetically real. No studio polish, no corporate backing—just four kids with guitars and something urgent to say.

Twenty-eight years later, when I'm diagnosing a cascade failure at 2:47 AM, I channel that same energy. Punk taught me that perfection is the enemy of getting things done. Sometimes you need three chords and the truth, not a symphony.

The Anti-Process Principle

Punk was born from rejecting the overly produced arena rock of the 1970s. In incident response, I've learned to reject the overly processed corporate response patterns that prioritize CYA over fixing the actual problem.

The punk approach to incidents:

  • No heroes - Punk rejected rock stars. Good incident response rejects the lone genius narrative. Everyone contributes.
  • Do it yourself - Don't wait for permission or the "right" tools. Use what you have. Fix it now.
  • Strip it down - Remove everything that doesn't serve the mission. No elaborate monitoring if it doesn't help you fix things faster.
  • Question authority - If the runbook says restart the server but you know it's a database lock, trust your instincts.

Basement Shows and War Rooms

I've been in incident war rooms that feel like corporate theater—twenty people on a call, half of them muted, reading from scripts. But the best incident responses I've seen feel like punk shows in someone's basement: intimate, intense, everyone essential.

Last month, we had a distributed denial-of-service attack that took down our API. Instead of spinning up the full incident response bureaucracy, three of us jumped into a room (literally—we work hybrid). No formal roles, no strict procedures, just:

"What do we know?"
"What can we try?"
"How do we know if it worked?"

We had the service back up in forty minutes. The post-incident review took longer than the actual incident.

The Beauty of Broken Things

Punk celebrates imperfection. A distorted guitar, a cracked voice, a drum machine that can't quite keep time—these aren't bugs, they're features. They create character, urgency, authenticity.

In incident response, I've stopped trying to hide the rough edges. When we communicate with users during outages, we don't use corporate-speak about "experiencing intermittent connectivity issues." We say "our stuff is broken and we're fixing it." Users appreciate the honesty, and it builds trust.

One of our biggest outages last year happened during a company all-hands meeting. Instead of crafting a polished incident communication, our CEO just stood up and said, "Our site is down. Brad's team is on it. We'll update you in an hour." Then he went back to talking about quarterly results.

No drama, no panic, just acknowledgment and commitment. Very punk.

Making a Scene (Not Drama)

Punk isn't about chaos—it's about focused energy and community. The DIY ethos built a network of clubs, labels, and zines that supported bands who couldn't get mainstream attention.

Good incident response creates the same kind of scene. We've built informal networks where people know who to call for specific expertise. Database locks? Call Sarah. Network weirdness? Text Miguel. Mystery performance degradation? Wake me up at 3 AM—I actually enjoy that debugging process.

This network exists outside the org chart. It's based on competence, trust, and the shared understanding that sometimes systems break and we all need to help each other fix them.

Straight Edge Monitoring

The straight edge movement within punk was about clarity and commitment—no alcohol, no drugs, no clouded judgment. I apply the same principle to monitoring and alerting.

Our monitoring is straight edge: no vanity metrics, no false positives, no alerts that don't require action. If it wakes you up, it better be worth waking up for. If it's not actionable, it's not an alert—it's noise.

We ruthlessly prune our dashboards. If a metric hasn't led to a fix in six months, we remove it. Clarity over completeness.

Never Mind the Bollocks (Or: Ignore the MTTR)

Corporate incident response loves metrics: Mean Time to Resolution, Mean Time to Detection, RCA completion rates. These metrics often become the mission instead of serving it.

Punk taught me to ignore metrics that don't serve users. I don't care if our MTTR looks bad if we prevented customer impact. I don't care about RCA completion rates if the real root cause is "sometimes things break and we fixed it."

The only metric that matters: did we help users faster this time than last time?

Formation of a New Scene

Punk wasn't just music—it was a philosophy that spread to art, fashion, politics, and zines. The DIY ethic influenced everything from independent record labels to community organizing.

I've tried to bring that same philosophy to our engineering culture. We run "chaos shows"—informal sessions where anyone can demo something they built, share something that broke, or propose something weird. No slides, no approval process, just show up and share.

These sessions have spawned some of our best tools and procedures. A five-minute demo of someone's personal debugging script becomes our new standard tool. A rant about slow deployments becomes a two-week improvement project.

The Encore

Punk shows don't really end—they just stop. The energy continues in the parking lot, in conversations that last until dawn, in connections that span decades.

The best incident responses have the same quality. The technical fix is just the beginning. The real value is in what we learn, how we share that knowledge, and how we build resilience for next time.

When young engineers ask me for career advice, I tell them to learn three things: how to code, how to debug, and how to think like a punk. The first two will get you a job. The third will help you build something that matters.

Now if you'll excuse me, our monitoring just detected an anomaly. Time to grab my (metaphorical) guitar and see what's broken.
