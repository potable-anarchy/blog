Surviving the Terrible Twos: Crisis Management

Last Tuesday, my two-year-old had a complete meltdown in the grocery store because I wouldn't let her open a package of crackers. The tantrum escalated quickly: screaming, throwing herself on the floor, and attracting the sympathetic (and judgmental) stares of other shoppers. As I calmly guided her through the crisis, I realized I was using the same techniques I'd learned from managing production incidents.

The Anatomy of a Crisis

Both toddler tantrums and system outages follow predictable patterns. There's usually a trigger event that seems minor to outside observers but represents a critical failure in the affected system. For my daughter, it was the crackers. For our production system last month, it was a single database query that started timing out.

In both cases, the initial problem quickly cascades. The toddler's frustration about the crackers becomes rage about everything—the shopping cart, the bright lights, the fact that she can't have candy. The database timeout becomes a cascade failure as connection pools exhaust, circuit breakers trip, and suddenly half the application is returning 500 errors.

Don't Panic, Don't Negotiate

The worst thing you can do during a toddler meltdown is panic or try to negotiate. "If you stop crying, I'll give you the crackers" teaches them that tantrums are an effective strategy. Similarly, the worst thing you can do during a production incident is make hasty changes or try to negotiate with the system—"maybe if we just restart this one service, everything will be fine."

Instead, I've learned to stay calm and systematic. With my daughter, I acknowledge her feelings ("You're upset about the crackers") without giving in to her demands. With production incidents, I acknowledge the problem ("The database is timing out") without immediately jumping to solutions.

The Comfort of Routine

Toddlers find security in predictable responses. When my daughter has a meltdown, I follow the same pattern every time: acknowledge her feelings, remove her from the situation if possible, and wait for the storm to pass. She knows what to expect, which helps her regain control faster.

Our incident response process follows the same principle. We have a documented runbook that starts with the same steps every time: acknowledge the incident, gather the right people, establish a communication channel, and begin systematic investigation. The team knows what to expect, which reduces stress and helps everyone think more clearly.

The Importance of Observation

With toddler tantrums, the key is learning to read the warning signs. My daughter gets a particular look in her eyes when she's approaching overload—usually when she's tired, hungry, or overstimulated. If I catch these signals early, I can often prevent the meltdown entirely.

Production systems give similar warning signs. Response times creep up, error rates tick higher, memory usage climbs steadily. The best incident management isn't about responding to crises—it's about preventing them by acting on early warning signals.

Communication During Chaos

When a toddler is mid-tantrum, they can't process complex information. Simple, clear communication works best: "I see you're upset. We're going to sit here until you feel better." Any attempt to explain why they can't have the crackers just adds more complexity to an already overwhelmed system.

During production incidents, stakeholders are often in a similar state of overwhelm. They don't need detailed technical explanations—they need clear, simple updates: "We've identified the problem. We're working on a fix. We'll update you in 15 minutes." Save the technical details for the post-mortem.

The Recovery Pattern

Toddler tantrums follow a predictable recovery pattern. The intensity peaks, then gradually subsides. My daughter will eventually stop crying, then usually want a hug. She's exhausted from the emotional outburst and needs comfort to fully reset.

System recovery follows similar patterns. Once we fix the immediate issue, there's usually a period of instability as caches warm up, connections re-establish, and everything settles back to normal. The team is also exhausted from the stress and needs time to decompress before they can effectively participate in a post-mortem.

Learning from Every Crisis

After each major tantrum, I try to understand what triggered it. Was she overtired? Did I miss the warning signs? Could I have structured the situation differently? These insights help me prevent future meltdowns or at least manage them more effectively.

Our incident post-mortems follow the same approach. We don't just fix the immediate problem—we try to understand the root cause, identify missed warning signs, and improve our processes. The goal isn't to eliminate all incidents (toddlers will have tantrums, systems will fail), but to get better at managing them.

The Support System

Having a support system makes all the difference. When my daughter has a public meltdown, I'm grateful for the understanding nod from other parents who've been there. When our production system fails, I'm grateful for the on-call team who jumps in to help without blame or judgment.

Crisis management isn't about being perfect—it's about having the right people, processes, and mindset to handle chaos when it inevitably occurs.

Building Resilience

The most important lesson from both toddler tantrums and production incidents is that resilience comes from practice, not perfection. My daughter is slowly learning to manage her emotions better, not because she never gets frustrated, but because she's experienced the pattern of upset-comfort-recovery enough times to trust that bad feelings pass.

Our engineering team has built similar resilience. We're not afraid of production incidents because we've handled them before. We know the process, we trust each other, and we've learned that systems can be fixed.

The Long Game

The goal isn't to eliminate all toddler tantrums or all production incidents—it's to build systems (both human and technical) that can handle stress gracefully. Some days my daughter makes it through the grocery store without a meltdown. Some days our production system handles traffic spikes without breaking. But when things do go wrong, we have the tools and experience to manage the crisis effectively.

The best crisis management happens long before the crisis occurs. It's the consistent routines, the clear communication patterns, and the trust that comes from weathering storms together.
