June 12, 2025 Incident Management

Toddler Chaos and Production Outages

At 2:47 AM, I'm awakened by screaming. Not from PagerDuty this time—from my 3-year-old who's discovered that his stuffed elephant isn't in the exact position he left it. As I stumble through the dark hallway, stepping on approximately seventeen toy cars, I realize this midnight crisis feels remarkably familiar.

"The system is DOWN!" he wails. "Ellie moved! Everything is BROKEN!"

Twenty minutes later, after carefully restoring Ellie the Elephant to her precise coordinates, implementing a pillow-based failover system, and conducting a thorough post-incident review ("Did the cat move Ellie? Did Ellie move herself?"), I'm back in bed. My phone buzzes—actual PagerDuty this time. As I open my laptop, I can't help but laugh at the parallel universes I'm navigating.

The Great Juice Box Incident of Tuesday Morning

Last Tuesday started like any production deployment. Everything seemed stable until 7:23 AM when my toddler discovered I'd given him the blue cup instead of the red cup. The meltdown was immediate and total—a cascading failure that took down breakfast, getting dressed, and any hope of making it to daycare on time.

"But you asked for the blue cup," I explained, showing him the conversation logs (okay, I just reminded him).

"NO! RED CUP! ALWAYS RED CUP!" he screamed, his tiny fists emphasizing each word.

This is when I realized: he'd implemented an unannounced breaking change to his cup API. No deprecation notice, no migration guide, just a hard cutover that broke all downstream dependencies (aka breakfast).

Incident Response: Toddler vs Production

The similarities are uncanny:

  • Alert fatigue is real - Whether it's the fifteenth "I'm not tired!" of bedtime or the fifteenth "CPU usage slightly elevated" of the night, you learn to differentiate critical from noise
  • Root cause is rarely obvious - "I don't want dinner" might actually mean "My sock feels weird" or "I saw a dog three hours ago and I'm still thinking about it"
  • Rollback isn't always possible - Once you've cut the sandwich into triangles, you cannot make it whole again. The damage is done. The trust is broken.
  • Communication is everything - "Use your words" applies equally to toddlers and that senior engineer who just types "broken" in Slack

The Sandbox Environment

My living room has become a literal sandbox environment. Last week, I watched my son conduct what can only be described as chaos engineering. He systematically tested the failure modes of every toy, discovering that Hot Wheels tracks make excellent dominoes and that Play-Doh can absolutely be load-tested into the carpet fibers.

"What are you doing?" I asked as he lined up every single toy car he owned.

"Making traffic," he explained. "Big, big traffic. Then CRASH!" He looked at me with the satisfied expression of a QA engineer who's just found a critical bug.

I've started applying his testing methodology to our systems. What happens if everything fails at once? What if we simulate maximum load then add chaos? Turns out, three-year-olds are natural chaos engineers—they just call it "playing."

Monitoring and Alerting

Toddlers come with built-in monitoring that would make Datadog jealous:

  • Real-time alerts for any state change ("MY SOCK FELL OFF!")
  • Predictive analytics ("I'm not tired" = crash imminent in T-minus 20 minutes)
  • Comprehensive logging (everything that happened today will be reported to grandma)
  • Zero false positives (if they're quiet, something is definitely wrong)

I've actually improved our production monitoring by asking myself: "Would a toddler notice this?" If a three-year-old can spot that one crayon is missing from a 64-pack, our monitoring should catch that one service that's responding 50ms slower than usual.

The Post-Incident Review

After the Great Juice Box Incident, we held a family retro. My son, now calm and sipping from his red cup, explained his perspective:

"Blue cup is for water. Red cup is for juice. Dada should know."

Fair point. We implemented several action items:

  • Clear documentation (drew pictures on the cups)
  • Stakeholder alignment (mommy was informed of the new protocol)
  • Automated testing (always confirm cup color before pouring)
  • Better change management (two-day notice required for cup protocol updates)

What I've Actually Learned

Parenting a toddler while managing production systems has taught me that chaos is the natural state of the universe. The best we can do is build resilient systems—whether that's a bedtime routine that can handle the unexpected appearance of a monster under the bed or a deployment pipeline that can survive when someone accidentally commits node_modules.

Both require patience, preparation, and the ability to remain calm when everything is on fire (sometimes literally, in the case of that toy fire truck with real sirens).

Most importantly, I've learned that whether you're debugging a production outage or figuring out why your toddler is crying, the answer is usually simpler than you think: something basic is broken, someone needs a nap, or you're overthinking it.

Now if you'll excuse me, I need to go. The monitoring system (my son) is alerting that the goldfish crackers are not arranged in the correct pattern. This is a P0 incident that requires immediate intervention.
