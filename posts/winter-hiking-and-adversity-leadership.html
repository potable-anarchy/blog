Winter Hiking and Adversity Leadership

The wind was howling at 40mph, snow was pelting our faces sideways, and the temperature had dropped to -15°F. We were three hours into what should have been a five-hour winter hike in the White Mountains, and two of my teammates were starting to show signs of hypothermia. As the trip leader, I had a choice: push forward to our planned campsite or make the hard call to turn back.

This moment, frozen and exhausted on Mount Washington's northern approach, taught me more about engineering leadership than any management book ever could. When systems fail and stakes are high, leadership isn't about having all the answers—it's about making clear decisions with incomplete information while keeping your team safe and motivated.

When Plans Meet Reality

We'd planned this winter hiking trip for months. Weather reports looked favorable, gear was checked and double-checked, and our route was conservative. But conditions can change fast in the mountains, just like they do in production systems. By noon, what started as light snow had turned into a full whiteout, and our planned 8-hour round trip was looking more like 12 hours in dangerous conditions.

I remember standing there with ice forming on my beard, watching my teammate Sarah struggle to zip her pack with numb fingers, and realizing this was exactly like those moments in engineering when your carefully planned deployment starts going sideways. You've got monitoring showing red, your team is stressed, and you need to decide: do you push forward with the original plan or cut your losses?

In both situations, the temptation is to stick with the plan because you've invested so much in it. We'd driven four hours to get to this trailhead. We'd been looking forward to this summit for weeks. Similarly, in engineering, you might have spent weeks preparing a migration or feature launch. But leadership means recognizing when conditions have changed enough that the original plan is no longer viable.

Reading Your Team Under Pressure

One of the most crucial skills I've developed, both on the trail and in engineering teams, is learning to read subtle signs of stress before they become critical failures. That day on Mount Washington, I noticed Sarah wasn't talking as much—usually a chatterbox, she'd gone quiet. Mike was fumbling with simple tasks like adjusting his pack straps. These weren't dramatic signals, but they were early indicators that the cold was affecting their cognitive function.

I see the same patterns in engineering teams during high-pressure incidents. The usually vocal team member goes quiet in Slack. Someone who normally catches typos starts making simple errors in commands. The key is recognizing these signals early, before they cascade into bigger problems.

When I called for a rest stop and pulled out hot tea from my thermos, it wasn't just about warming up—it was about creating space to assess where everyone was mentally and physically. In incident management, this is like calling for a brief sync during a long debugging session. It's not wasted time; it's essential maintenance for your team's capacity to think clearly.

Transparent Communication in Crisis

As we huddled behind a boulder to escape the wind, I had to make a leadership decision I'd seen replicated countless times in engineering: how much information to share with the team when things are going wrong. Do you maintain optimism to keep morale up, or do you share the full gravity of the situation?

I chose transparency: "Look, conditions are way worse than forecasted. We're burning more energy than planned fighting this wind, and it's only going to get worse as we gain elevation. I think we should turn back." Some leaders might worry this would demoralize the team, but I've learned that people perform better when they understand the full context.

The response was immediate relief. Mike admitted he'd been worried about the same thing but didn't want to be the one to suggest turning back. Sarah said she'd been struggling more than she wanted to let on. This is so similar to what happens in engineering teams during outages—junior engineers often hesitate to voice concerns about an approach because they assume the senior people have it under control.

Creating psychological safety where people can voice concerns early is crucial for both mountain safety and system reliability. The cost of ego is much higher than the cost of changing course.

Decision Frameworks Under Uncertainty

The decision to turn back wasn't emotional—it was systematic. We had clear criteria for success (reach the summit safely) and clear risk factors (deteriorating weather, team energy levels, time remaining). When the risk factors started outweighing our ability to achieve success safely, the decision became obvious.

I use the same framework for engineering decisions during incidents. What are our success criteria? What are the risk factors? At what point do the risks outweigh the benefits of continuing with the current approach? Having these frameworks established before you're in crisis mode makes decisions much clearer when adrenaline is high and time is short.

That day, our success criteria weren't just "reach the summit"—they were "reach the summit and get everyone back safely with adequate reserves." When those became mutually exclusive, the choice was clear. Similarly, in engineering, success isn't just "ship the feature"—it's "ship the feature without compromising system stability or team wellbeing."

Maintaining Team Cohesion During Retreat

The hike back down was where real leadership mattered. It would have been easy for the team to get demoralized about "failing" to reach our goal. Instead, I focused on reframing the mission. We weren't retreating—we were making a smart tactical decision that kept us available for future adventures.

I made sure to point out what we'd done well: our gear choices had been spot-on, we'd maintained good communication throughout, and most importantly, we'd recognized changing conditions and adapted appropriately. These were all skills that would serve us on future, more ambitious trips.

This reframing is essential in engineering teams too. When you have to roll back a deployment or cancel a feature launch, it's easy for the team to feel like they've failed. But good leadership helps them see what they learned, what processes worked well, and how the experience makes them stronger for future challenges.

By the time we reached the parking lot, the team was already talking about our next winter hiking attempt, analyzing what we'd learned and how we could apply it. That's the mark of a resilient team—they bounce back from setbacks and get stronger.

Building Antifragile Teams

The best winter hiking teams don't just survive difficult conditions—they get stronger from them. They develop better judgment, stronger trust, and more robust decision-making processes. The same is true for engineering teams that handle incidents well.

Six months later, I led the same team back to Mount Washington in similar conditions. This time, we made it to the summit and back safely. The difference wasn't just better luck with weather—it was that we'd learned to read conditions more accurately, communicate more effectively, and make decisions more quickly. We'd become an antifragile team.

The key insight from that first winter hiking experience is that leadership during adversity isn't about being the strongest or most experienced person on the team. It's about creating environments where people can perform their best under pressure, where information flows freely, and where the team can make collective decisions that prioritize long-term success over short-term wins.

Whether you're navigating a blizzard on Mount Washington or debugging a production outage at 3 AM, the principles are the same: stay calm, communicate clearly, make decisions based on data rather than emotion, and always prioritize the long-term health of your team over immediate goals. The summit will still be there tomorrow, and so will the opportunity to build something great with a team that trusts each other.
